✨ Third order non‑linear effects arise when the polarisation of a material depends on the third power of the applied electric field.
Consider an electric field composed of three distinct frequency components:
✦
\(E(t)\) \(=\) \(\Re\{E(\omega_1) \exp(j \omega_1 t)\}\) \(+\) \(\Re\{E(\omega_2) \exp(j \omega_2 t)\}\) \(+\) \(\Re\{E(\omega_3) \exp(j \omega_3 t)\}\)
This can be written more compactly using positive and negative frequency indices:
✦
\(E(t)\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{q = \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3} \frac{1}{2} E(\omega_q) \exp(i \omega_q t)\)
where \(\omega_{-q} = -\omega_q\) and \(E(-\omega_q) = E^*(\omega_q)\).
The non‑linear polarisation for a third‑order process is often written in a simplified form as:
✦
\(P_{NL}\) \(=\) \(4 \chi^{(0)} \epsilon^3\)
Substituting the full field expression into the third‑order polarisation yields:
✦
\(P_{NL}\) \(=\) \(\frac{1}{8} \chi^{(3)} \sum E(\omega_q) E(\omega_1) E(\omega_2) \exp\bigl(j (\omega_1 + \omega_2 + \omega_3) t\bigr)\)
The summation runs over all combinations of the input frequencies, generating new frequencies such as: \(\omega_1,\; 3\omega_1,\; 2\omega_2,\; \dots\) etc.
For an efficient energy transfer between waves, two conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:
Frequency matching condition
✦
\(\omega_1\) \(+\) \(\omega_2\) \(=\) \(\omega_3\) \(+\) \(\omega_4\)
Phase matching condition
✦
\(k_1\) \(+\) \(k_2\) \(=\) \(k_3\) \(+\) \(k_4\)
These conditions ensure that the non-linear polarisation drives the generated waves constructively over the entire interaction length.

Third-Order non-linear Coefficient (\(\chi^{(3)}\))

The third-order susceptibility \(\chi^{(3)}\) is observed in:
  • Glasses
  • Crystals
  • Semiconductors
  • Organic materials
Typical values:
✦
\(d\) \(\sim\) \(10^{-34} \; \text{c/v}^2\) \(\text{ to }\) \(10^{-29} \; \text{c/v}^2\)
Even though \(d\) is small, the third-order power dependence is higher than that of second-order effects, so it is not considered negligible.

Third Order Non-linear Effect

Centro symmetric media
\(P\) will reverse as \(E\) is reversed.
✦
\(P_{NL}\) \(=\) \(4 \chi^{(3)} E^3\)

Kerr Effect

Self-induced effect in which the phase velocity of a wave depends on its own intensity.
Kerr medium responds to an optical field by generating third harmonics, as well as sum and difference frequencies from triplets of frequencies.

Optical Kerr Effect

✦
\(P_{NL} (\omega)\) \(=\) \(3 \chi^{(3)} |E(\omega)|^2 E(\omega)\)
✦
\(\varepsilon_0 \Delta \chi\) \(=\) \(\frac{P_{NL}(\omega)}{E(\omega)}\) \(=\) \(3 \chi^{(3)} |E(\omega)|^2\) \(=\) \(6 \chi^{(2)} \eta I\)
As intensity increases, \(\Delta \chi\) increases, leading to a change in the refractive index.
✦
\(\Delta n\) \(=\) \(\frac{\Delta \chi}{2n}\)

What is \(\chi\) as a Tensor?

\(\chi^{(3)}\) is a fourth-rank tensor \(\chi_{ijkl}^{(3)}\). It connects the polarization \(P_i^{(3)}\) to the product of electric fields \(E_j E_k E_l\). The tensor nature describes how the non-linear response depends on the direction of fields and crystal orientation.
✦
\(I\) \(=\) \(\frac{|E(\omega)|^2}{\eta}\)
The non-linear Kerr effect can act as a lens (self-focusing or self-defocusing) due to intensity-dependent refractive index.
✦
\(\Delta n\) \(=\) \(\frac{\Delta \chi}{2n}\) \(=\) \(\frac{3 \chi^{(3)} I}{n^2 \varepsilon_0 c}\) \(=\) \(n_2 I\)
✦
\(n_2\) \(=\) \(\frac{3 \chi^{(3)}}{n^2 \varepsilon_0 c}\)
\(n_2\) is the non-linear refractive index coefficient. It describes how the refractive index changes with light intensity: \(n = n_0 + n_2 I\), where \(n_0\) is the linear refractive index. A positive \(n_2\) causes self-focusing (beam narrows), while a negative \(n_2\) causes self-defocusing (beam spreads).
Kerr-effect equation:
✦
\(n(I)\) \(=\) \(n + n_2 I\)
Typical values of \(n_2\):
  • \(10^{-16}-10^{-19}\), glasses
  • \(10^{-14}-10^{-7}\), doped glasses
  • \(10^{-10}-10^{-8}\), organic materials
  • \(10^{-10}-10^{-2}\), semi-conductors
\(n_2\) is sensitive to \(\lambda\) and also depends on polarization.

Self phase modulation (SPM)

Phase shift measured by an optical beam of power \(P\) and cross section \(A\) travelling a distance \(L\) in the medium:
✦
\(\phi\) \(=\) \(n(I) \cdot \left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\right)L\)
✦
\(\phi\) \(=\) \(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \left[ n + n_2 I \right]L\)
✦
\(\phi\) \(=\) \(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \left[ n + n_2 \frac{P}{A} \right]L\)
✦
\(\Delta \phi\) \(=\) \(\frac{2 \pi n_2 L P}{\lambda A}\)
Given: \(L = 1\,\text{m}\), \(A = 10^{-2}\,\text{mm}^2\), \(n_2 = 10^{-10}\,\text{cm}^2/\text{W}\), \(P = 0.5\), \(\Delta \phi = \pi\), find \(\lambda\).
✦
\(\lambda\) \(=\) \(\frac{\Delta \phi A}{2 \pi n_2 L P}\)
✦
\(\lambda\) \(=\) \(\frac{A}{2 n_2 L P}\)
✦
\(\lambda\) \(=\) \(1\,\mu\text{m}\)

Self Focusing

Phase modulation can be converted to intensity modulation.
A weak optical beam may be controlled by an intense optical beam.
Self focusing is associated with self-phase modulation, where an intense beam transmits through or induces a shift in the non-linear material.
The refractive index change mimics the intensity pattern of the beam.
If the beam has its highest intensity at the centre, the maximum change in refractive index also occurs at the centre.
The non-linear sheet then acts as a graded-index medium.
This imparts a non-uniform phase shift to the wave, causing wavefront curvature.
The medium can act as a lens with a power-dependent focal length.

Soliton

Wave front that propagates long distance without change.

Spatial Soliton

Occurs in the time domain when group velocity dispersion is compensated by SPM.
If it is time, it is called temporal; if it is space, it is called spatial.
Intense optical beam travels through substantial thickness of non‑linear medium.
Refractive index is altered non‑uniformly.
Medium acts as a graded index waveguide, maximum at maxima.
Beam can create its own waveguide.
If the intensity of the beam has the same spatial distribution in the transverse plane as one of the modes of the waveguide that the beam itself creates, the beam propagates self‑consistently without changing its spatial distribution.
Diffraction is compensated by self phase modulation (SPM).
Self-guided beam - that is called spatial soliton.
Self guiding of light in optical Kerr medium.
Helmholtz equation:
✦
\(\Delta^2 + n(I) k_0^2 E\) \(=\) \(0\)
✦
\(n(I)\) \(=\) \(n + n_2 I\)
✦
\(k_0\) \(=\) \(\frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}\) \(=\) \(\frac{\omega}{c}\)
✦
\(I\) \(=\) \(\frac{|E|^2}{2 \eta}\)
Input electric field propagating in the \(z\)-direction:
✦
\(E\) \(=\) \(A(x,z) \exp(-j k z)\)
The wavevector \(k\) is related to the linear refractive index \(n\):
✦
\(k\) \(=\) \(n k_0\)
Here, \(A\) is a function of both transverse coordinate \(x\) and propagation distance \(z\) (slowly varying envelope approximation).
Applying the paraxial approximation, the second derivative simplifies to:
✦
\(\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2} \left[ A \exp(-j k z) \right]\) \(\simeq\) \(\left(-2 j k \frac{\partial A}{\partial z} - k^2 A\right) \exp(-j k z)\)
Substituting into the wave equation with intensity-dependent refractive index \(n(I)\) yields the non-linear Schrödinger equation:
✦
\(\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial x^2} - 2j k \frac{\partial A}{\partial z} + k^2 \left[ \frac{n^2(I)}{n^2} - 1 \right] A\) \(=\) \(0\)
✦
\(\left(n^2(I) - n^2\right)\) \(=\) \(\left[n(I) + n\right]\left[n(I) - n\right]\) \(=\) \(n_2 I (2n)\) \(=\) \(\frac{2 n_2 n |A|^2}{2\eta}\) \(=\) \(\frac{n_2 n |A|^2}{\eta}\)
Non-linear Schrödinger equation:
✦
\(\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial x^2} + \frac{n_2}{\eta_0} k^2 |A|^2 A\) \(=\) \(2j k \frac{\partial A}{\partial z}\)
\(A(x, z=0)\), \(A(x, z=L)\)

Third Harmonic Generation

In a monochromatic optical field:
✦
\(P_{NL} = 4 \chi^{(3)} E^3 \tag{1}\)
✦
\(E(t) = \Re \{E(\omega) \exp(j \omega t)\} \tag{2}\)
From (1) and (2):
✦
\(p_{NL}(\omega) = 3 \chi^{(3)} |E(\omega)|^2 E(\omega) \tag{3}\)
✦
\(P_{NL}(3\omega) = \chi^{(3)} E^{3}(\omega) \tag{4}\)
Equation (4) describes Third Harmonic Generation (THG); the conversion efficiency is low.
THG is often achieved via SHG followed by SFG of fundamental and second harmonic wave.

Applications of Third-Order Non-linearity

✦
\(\vec{P}\) \(\propto\) \(|\vec{E}|^3\)
Third Harmonic Generation
A monochromatic wave is frequency-tripled as it passes through the medium, the output is at thrice the input frequency.
Self Phase Modulation
Intensity-dependent refractive index of a medium causes a propagating optical pulse to modulate its own phase.
Self Focusing
An intense optical beam modifies the refractive index of the medium, creating a graded-index waveguide that focuses the beam itself, counteracting diffraction.
Four Wave Mixing
Interaction of three optical waves in a third-order non-linear medium generates a fourth wave at a frequency \(\omega_4 = \omega_1 + \omega_2 + \omega_3\) or \(\omega_4 = \omega_1 + \omega_2 - \omega_3\), enabling wavelength conversion and parametric amplification.
Phase Conjugation
A non-linear process that generates a wave with the phase conjugate of the input wave, allowing compensation of optical distortions and aberration correction in imaging systems.

Cross Phase Modulation (XPM)

Cross phase modulation is a non=linear effect where one wave controls the phase of another wave.
Input consists of two waves:
✦
\(E(t)\) \(=\) \(\mathrm{Re}\{ E(\omega_1) e^{j \omega_1 t}\} + \mathrm{Re}\{E(\omega_2) e^{j \omega_2 t}\}\)
Polarization in a non-linear medium for third-order non-linearity:
✦
\(P_{\text{NL}}\) \(=\) \(4 \chi^{(3)} E^3\)
The non-linear polarization at frequency \(\omega_1\) includes contributions from both waves:
✦
\(P_{\text{NL}}(\omega_1)\) \(=\) \(\chi^{(3)} \left[ 3 |E(\omega_1)|^2 + 6|E(\omega_2)|^2 \right] E(\omega_1)\)
This can be expressed in terms of the change in susceptibility:
✦
\(P_{\text{NL}}(\omega_1)\) \(=\) \(2 \varepsilon_0 n \Delta \chi E(\omega_1)\)
The intensity-dependent change in refractive index is:
✦
\(\Delta n\) \(=\) \(n_2 (I_1 + 2 I_2)\)
where \(I_1\) is the intensity of the first wave and \(I_2\) is the intensity of the second wave.
Physical meaning: When two waves propagate through a non-linear medium, we can use the intensity of one wave to modulate the phase of the other wave.
The phase change is given by:
✦
\(\Delta \phi\) \(=\) \(2\pi\) \(\lambda\) \(\Delta n L\)
Therefore, wave 1 travels with an effective index \(n + \Delta n\) controlled by its own intensity as well as that of wave 2.
Similarly, wave 2 encounters a similar effect. Thus, the two waves are coupled.
This coupling can result in contamination of information between optical communication channels at neighbouring frequencies in wavelength division multiplexing (WDM).
