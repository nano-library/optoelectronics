Sum Frequency Generation (SFG)

Sum frequency generation is a second-order nonlinear process where two input photons of frequencies \(\omega_1\) and \(\omega_2\) combine to produce a single output photon at the sum frequency \(\omega_3 = \omega_1 + \omega_2\).

Frequency Matching and Phase Matching

If a wave has an electric field as a function of frequency \(\omega\), frequency matching ensures energy conservation (\(\omega_3 = \omega_1 + \omega_2\)), while phase matching (\(\Delta k = k_3 - k_1 - k_2 = 0\)) ensures constructive interference of the nonlinear polarization along the propagation direction for efficient energy transfer.
✦
✦ Electric Field (Wave 1)
\(E(\omega_1)\) \(=\) \(A_1 \, e^{- j \, \vec{k}_1 \cdot \vec{r}}\)
✦
✦ Electric Field (Wave 2)
\(E(\omega_2)\) \(=\) \(A_2 \, e^{- j \, \vec{k}_2 \cdot \vec{r}}\)
where
\(e^{-j \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}}\) - spatial phase factor
✦
✦
\(P_{NL} (\omega_3)\) \(=\) \(2 \, d \, E(\omega_1) \, E(\omega_2) \)
✦
✦
\(P_{NL} (\omega_3)\) \(=\) \(2 \, d \, A_1 \, A_2 \, e^{- j \, \overrightarrow{k_3} \, \overrightarrow{r}}\)
✦
✦ Frequency Matching Condition
\(\omega_1\) \(+\) \(\omega_2\) \(=\) \(\omega_3\)
✦
✦ Phase Matching Condition
\(\overrightarrow{k_1}\) \(+\) \(\overrightarrow{k_2}\) \(=\) \(\overrightarrow{k_3}\)
Medium acts as a light source of \(\omega_3 = \omega_1 + \omega_2\) with complex amplitude proportional to \(e^{-j \, \overrightarrow{k_3} \, \overrightarrow{r}}\), where (\(\overrightarrow{k_3} = \overrightarrow{k_1} + \overrightarrow{k_2}\))
Argument of complex wave function is (\(\omega t - \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{r}\)).
(\(\omega_1 + \omega_2 = \omega_3\)) ensures both temporal and spatial phase matching.
physical meaning is to sustain mutual interaction over extended duration of time and region of space.

Optical Frequency Conversion

Optical frequency conversion is a nonlinear process in which input light at one or more frequencies is converted to output light at a different frequency via interaction with a nonlinear medium.
✦
✦ Up Conversion
\(\omega_3\) \(=\) \(\omega_1\) \(-\) \(\omega_2\)
SHG is a degenerate special case of SFG. If \(\omega_1 = \omega_2 = \omega\), then \(\omega_3 = 2 \; \omega\)
✦
✦ Down Conversion
\(\omega_2\) \(=\) \(\omega_3\) \(-\) \(\omega_1\)

Applications

  • To generate coherent light at wavelength where no adequete lasers are available.
  • Optical mixers in optical communication system.

Optical Parametric Amplifiers (OPA)

When wave 1 (signal) interacts with wave 3 (pump) in a nonlinear medium, wave 1 is amplified and wave 2 (idler) is generated at the difference frequency \(\omega_2 = \omega_3 - \omega_1\). This device operates as a coherent amplifier at frequency \(\omega_1\). The gain depends on the power of the pump wave.

Applications

  • Amplifing the signal.
  • Helps in detecting small signals by amplifying them.

Optical Parametric Oscillator (OPO)

An optical parametric oscillator is formed by placing an optical parametric amplifier (OPA) inside a resonant cavity to provide proper feedback. Only the pump wave is supplied externally; the signal and idler waves are generated and sustained through oscillation when the gain exceeds the cavity losses.

Applications

  • Used for generation of coherent light.
  • Mode locked pulse trains over a continuous range of frequency.
  • If there is no tunable laser, we can use OPOs.

Spontaneous Parametric Down Conversion (SPDC)

Only the pump wave (wave 3) is supplied as input. Through second-order non-linear interaction, the pump photon spontaneously down-converts into two lower-frequency photons, wave 1 (signal) and wave 2 (idler), satisfying \(\omega_3 = \omega_1 + \omega_2\) and phase-matching conditions. This process is a key source of entangled photon pairs for quantum optics.
✦
\(\omega_1\) \(+\) \(\omega_2\) \(=\) \(\omega_3\)
✦
\(k_1\) \(+\) \(k_2\) \(=\) \(k_3\)
The coupled frequency and phase-matching conditions lead to multiple solutions. Each solution forms a pair of waves (wave 1 and wave 2) with specific frequency \(\omega\) and propagation direction \(k\).

\(\eta_{SHG}\) \(=\) \(C\) \(\cdot\) \(L^2\) \(A\) \(\cdot\) \(P(\omega)\)

\(P\) - incident power
\(A\) - cross sectional area of interaction volume
L - interaction length
\(c\) - speed of light

Wave Guide Structure

Waveguides maximize SHG efficiency by reducing beam area (A) and increasing interaction length (L).
Waveguides provide an optimal platform for second harmonic generation (SHG) by confining light to small cross-sectional areas over extended propagation distances. The efficiency \(\eta_{\text{SHG}}\) scales as \(\frac{L^2}{A}\), making waveguides ideal for enhancing non-linear interactions.

How Waveguide Parameters Enhance SHG Efficiency

  • Decrease in beam area (A),tighter confinement increases intensity, directly boosting non-linear polarization
  • Increase in interaction length (L), longer propagation enhances phase-matched conversion efficiency

Common Waveguide Types

  • Planar waveguide
  • Channel waveguide
  • Optical fiber
The efficiency scaling \(\eta_{\text{SHG}} \propto \frac{L^2}{A}\) explains why guided-wave geometries outperform bulk crystals for SHG, especially with pulsed lasers where peak intensities are high.
High intensity requires high incident power; pulsed lasers provide the necessary peak intensities for non-linear effects.

Second-Harmonic Generation in Silica Glass Fiber

Due to the centrosymmetric nature of the medium, second-harmonic generation (SHG) is not observed in pristine silica glass fiber.

Applications of SHG

SHG is observed under the following conditions:
  • Electric quadrupole (i.e., magnetic dipole) interactions
  • Defects in the color center of the fiber core
  • Dielectric crystals
  • Semiconductors
  • Organic materials
