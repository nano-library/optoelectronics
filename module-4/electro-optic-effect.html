✨ Electro-optic effect represents a change in the refractive index of the material that results from the application of a steady or low-frequency electric field.
When a dielectric medium is subjected to a low-frequency (or static) electric field, its refractive index tensor undergoes a subtle yet controlled distortion. This phenomenon, known as the linear electro-optic effect (Pockels effect), allows us to modulate the phase, frequency, or polarization of light using an electrical signal.
✦
✦ Total Optical Field
\(E(t)\) \(=\) \(E(0)\) \(+\) \(Re\{E(\omega) \; e^{j \, \omega \, t}\}\)
where
\(E(0)\) - steady component (\(\omega = 0\))
\(E(\omega) \; e^{j \omega t}\) - harmonic component at angular frequency \(\omega\)

Non-linear Polarization and the Origin of Index Modulation

When an intense optical field propagates through a non-centrosymmetric crystal, the induced polarization \(P\) no longer depends linearly on the electric field. The leading non-linear contribution is quadratic in the field:
✦
✦ Non-linear Polarization
\(P_{NL}\) = \(d \; E^2\)
where
\(d\) - non-linear coefficient (proportional to \(\chi^{(2)}\))
\(E\) - total applied field
Substituting the decomposed field \(E(t)\) into the quadratic expression yields a spectrum of frequency components. After expanding and collecting terms, we obtain:
✦
\(P_{NL} (t)\) = \(P_{NL} (0)\) + \(Re\{ P_{NL}(\omega)\; e^{j \, \omega \, t}\} \) + \(Re \{ P_{NL} (2 \, \omega) \; e^{j \, 2 \, \omega \, t} \}\)
where
\(P_{NL}(0)\) = \(d [2 E^2(0) + |E(\omega)|^2] \)
\(P_{NL}(\omega)\) = \(4d E(0) E(\omega)\)
\(P_{NL}(2 \omega)\) = \( d E^2(\omega)\)
When the optical field is very small, \(|E(\omega)|^2 \ll |E(0)|^2\), the term \(P_{NL}(2\omega)\) becomes negligible. This is precisely the condition for the linear electro-optic effect (Pockels effect), the optical field is weak enough that it does not significantly deplete itself, yet it experiences a refractive index change proportional to the applied DC field \(E(0)\).
Under this approximation, the dominant dynamic term is:
✦
✦ Electro-Optic Polarization
\(P_{NL}(\omega)\) = \(4d \; E(0) \; E(\omega)\)
This expression gives a linear proportionality between the non-linear polarization at frequency \(\omega\) and the optical field \(E(\omega)\) itself:
✦
\(P_{NL}(\omega)\) \(\propto\) \(E(\omega)\)
Writing the total polarization at frequency \(\omega\) as \(P(\omega) = \varepsilon_0 \, \chi \, E(\omega)+P_{NL}(\omega)\), we identify an induced change \(\Delta \chi\) such that:
✦
✦ Induced Susceptibility Change
\(P_{NL}(\omega)\) = \(\epsilon_0 \, \Delta \chi \, E(\omega)\)
Equating this with \(4 \, d \, E(0) \, E(\omega)\) gives:
✦
✦ Linear Electro-Optic Coefficient
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\Delta}}\chi\) = \(4 \; d\) \(\epsilon_0\) \(E(0)\)
As \(\Delta \chi\) increases linearly with the applied DC field \(E(0)\), the refractive index changes accordingly. This is the electro-optic effect: an electric field controls the optical properties of the medium in real time.

From Susceptibility to Refractive Index Change

The connection between a material's polarization and its optical properties is expressed through the refractive index. In a linear, isotropic medium, the relation is:
✦
✦ Refractive Index Relation
\(n^2\) \(=\) \(1\) \(+\) \(\chi\)
However, when an external electric field \(E(0)\) is applied, the susceptibility changes by \(\Delta \chi\). This induces a corresponding change in the refractive index. To find the relationship, we differentiate both sides:
✦
✦ Index-Susceptibility Relation
\(2n \, {\color{#F08FB0}{\Delta}}n\) \(=\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\Delta}}\chi\)
Using the field-induced susceptibility change, which is given by Equation named Linear Electro-Optic Coefficient.
✦
✦ Field-Induced Refractive Index Change
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\Delta}}n\) = \(2 \; d\) \(n \; \varepsilon_0\) \(E(0)\)
When an external electric field \(E(0)\) is applied to the crystal, the refractive index changes from \(n\) to \(n + \Delta n\). This means the non-linear nature of the medium creates a direct coupling between the applied DC field \(E(0)\) and the optical field \(E(\omega)\). The electric field controls the optical field.

Pockel's Effect

Named after Friedrich Carl Alwin Pockels, who first described the phenomenon in 1893, the Pockels effect (or linear electro-optic effect) refers to the linear dependence of the refractive index change on the applied electric field. Unlike the Kerr effect (quadratic in \(E\)), the Pockels effect is linear:
✦
✦ Index Change
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\Delta}}n\) \(=\) \(1\) \(2\) \(n^3 \, \tau \, E(0)\)
where
\(\tau\) - Pockels coefficient (linear electro-optic coefficient) [m/V]

Relationship Between \(d\) and the Pockels Coefficient \(\tau\)

We can now connect the microscopic non-linear coefficient \(d\) (from \(P_{NL}=d \, E^2\)) to the macroscopic Pockels coefficient \(\tau\).
✦
✦ Pockels Coefficient in Terms of \(d\)
\(\tau\) = \(4 \, d\) \(\varepsilon \, n^4\)
