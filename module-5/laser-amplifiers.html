The probability density (\(s^{-1}\)) that an unexcited atom absorbs a single photon is
✦
\(W_i\) \(=\) \(\phi\) \(\sigma(\nu)\)
where
\(\phi\) - Photon flux, photons per second per area
\(\sigma(\nu)\) - Transition cross section at the frequency \(\nu\)
✦
\(\sigma\) \(=\) \(\lambda^2\) \(8 \, \pi \, t_{\text{sp}}\) \(g(\nu)\)
where
\(\lambda\) - Wavelength of light in the medium.
\(t_{\text{sp}}\) - Effective spontaneous lifetime for stimulated emission
\(\nu\) - Frequency
\(g(\nu)\) - Normalized lineshape function
Probability Density
The probability density for stimulated emission is the same as that for absorption.

Electro-Optic Amplitude Modulation

The average density of absorbed photons (number of photons per unit time per unit volume) is \( N_1 W_i\).
The average density of clone photons generated as a result of stimulated emission is \(N_2 W_i\).
The net number of photons gained per second per unit volume is therefore \(N W_i\), where \(N = N_2 - N_1\) is the population density difference, which is often simply referred to as the population difference.
If \(N\) is positive, a population inversion exists, in which case the medium can act as an amplifier and the photon-flux density of a wave passing through the medium can increase.
If \(N\) is negative, the medium acts as an attenuator and the photon-flux density decreases.
If \(N = 0\), the medium is transparent.
Lasers and Amplifiers
All lasers are amplifiers, but not all amplifiers are lasers.
Since the incident photons travel in the \(z\) direction, the stimulated-emission photons also travel in that direction.
An external pump providing a population inversion (N > 0) then causes the photon-flux density \(\phi(z)\) to increase with \(z\). Because emitted photons stimulate further emissions, the growth at any position \(z\) is proportional to the population at that position; \(\phi(z)\) thus increases exponentially.
Consider an incremental cylinder of length dz and unit area. If \(\phi(z)\) and \(\phi(z) + d\phi(z)\) are the photon-flux densities entering and exiting the incremental cylinder, respectively, then \(d\phi(z)\) must be the photon-flux density emitted from within the cylinder.
This incremental number of photons per unit area per unit time, \(d\phi(z)\), is simply the number of photons gained per unit time per unit volume, \(NW_i\), multiplied by the thickness of the cylinder \(dz\):
✦
\(d\phi(z)\) \(=\) \(NW_i dz\)
It can be written in the form of a differential equation,
✦
\(d\phi(z)\) \(dz\) \(=\) \(\gamma (\nu) \phi (z)\)
✦
\(\gamma(\nu)\) \(=\) \(N \sigma(\nu)\)
✦
\(\gamma(\nu)\) \(=\) \(N\) \(\lambda^2\) \(8 \, \pi \, t_{\text{sp}}\) \(g(\nu)\)
The coefficient \(\gamma(\nu)\) represents the net gain in the photon-flux density per unit length of the medium.
The solution of differen equation is the exponentially increasing function
✦
\(\phi(z)\) \(=\) \(\phi(0) \, e^{\gamma(\nu) z}\)
Since the optical intensity \(I(z) = h \nu \phi(z)\), it can also be written in terms of \(I\) as
✦
\(I(z)\) \(=\) \(I(0) \, e^{\gamma(\nu) z}\)
Thus, \(\gamma(\nu)\) also represents the gain in the intensity per unit length of the medium.
The amplifier gain coefficient \(\gamma (\nu)\) is seen to be proportional to the population difference \(N = N_2 - N_1\).
✦
\(\gamma(\nu)\) \(\propto\) \(N\)
Though \(N\) was taken to be positive in the example provided above, the derivation is valid whatever the sign of \(N\). In the absence of a population inversion, N is negative (\(N2 < N1 \)) and so too is the gain coefficient.
The medium will then attenuate (rather than amplify) light traveling in the \(z\) direction, in accordance with the exponentially decreasing function,
✦
\(\phi(z)\) \(=\) \(\phi(0) \, e^{- \alpha(\nu) z}\)
where
\(\alpha(\nu) = - \gamma (v)\) - Attenuation coefficient

Bandwidth

The dependence of the gain coefficient \(\gamma(\nu)\) on the frequency of the incident light \(\nu\) is contained in its proportionality to the lineshape function \(g(\nu)\), as given in (15.14).
\[ \gamma (\nu) \propto g(\nu) \]
\(g(c)\) is function of \(\Delta \nu\) width about atomic resonance frequency.

Laser Amplifier

Since stimulated emission and absorption are governed by the atomic transition, the laser amplifier is a resonant device, with a resonance frequency and bandwidth determined by the lineshape function of the atomic transition.
The linewidth \(\Delta \nu\) is measured either in units of frequency (Hz) or in units of wavelength (nm).
\[ \Delta \lambda = \left| \Delta \left(\frac{c}{\nu}\right) \right| = \frac{c}{\nu^2} \Delta v = \frac{\lambda_0^2}{c}\Delta \nu \] Example Thus, a linewidth Δν = 1 THz at λo = 0.6 μm corresponds to Δλ = 1.2 nm.

Line Shape Function

If the lineshape function is Lorentzian,
\[ g(\nu) = \frac{\frac{\Delta \nu}{2 \pi}}{(\nu-\nu_0)^2 + \left( \frac{\Delta \nu}{2} \right)^2} \]
The gain coefficient is then also Lorentzian with the same width, i.e.,
\[ \gamma = \gamma(\nu_0) \frac{\left(\frac{\Delta \nu}{2}\right)^2}{(\nu-\nu_0)^2 + \left( \frac{\Delta \nu}{2} \right)^2} \]

Amplifier Pumping

External Source of power to provide the enrgy required to amplify.
Laser amplifiers require an external source of power to provide the energy required to augment the input signal. The pump supplies this power via a mechanism that excites the electrons in the atoms, causing them to move from lower to higher atomic energy levels.
To achieve amplification, the pump must provide a population inversion on the transition of interest
\[ N = N_2 - N_1 > 0 \]
Pumping may be achieved in many ways, optically: Flash lamp, laser, Electrically: gas discharge, electron beam, ion beam, Chemically.

Continuous Wave (CW) Operation

Rates of excitation and decay of various levels (energy) must be balanced to maintain steady state, \((2) \rightarrow (1)\) transition.
